SLIDE 02

What is Cognitive Development?

  • Content:
  • Cognition: The activity of knowing and the processes through which knowledge is acquired
  • Cognitive development: Changes in mental activities including:
    • Attending, perceiving, learning, thinking, remembering
    • Piaget's Definition of Intelligence:
  • A basic life function enabling adaptation to environment
  • Cognitive equilibrium: Balanced relationship between thought processes and environment
  • Children are constructivists – they actively build their own knowledge
SLIDE 03

Piaget's Key Concepts – How Knowledge Develops

ConceptDefinitionExample
SchemeOrganized pattern of thought/actionA baby's sucking scheme
OrganizationCombining schemes into complex systemsCombining gazing, reaching, grasping
AdaptationAdjusting to environmental demandsLearning from experience
AssimilationInterpreting new experiences using existing schemesCalling a horse "doggie"
AccommodationModifying schemes for new experiencesLearning "horse" is different from "dog"

  • Assimilation and accommodation work together to promote cognitive growth
  • End result: Adaptation and cognitive equilibrium

SLIDE 05

Sensorimotor Stage (0–2 Years)

AgeAchievement
0–1 moReflex activity – exercising innate reflexes
1–4 moPrimary circular reactions – repeating body-centered actions
4–8 moSecondary circular reactions – repeating actions on external objects
8–12 moCoordination of secondary schemes – first intentional, goal-directed behavior
12–18 moTertiary circular reactions – active experimentation
18–24 moMental combinations – inner experimentation, insight

  • Object Permanence
  • Understanding objects exist when not visible
  • A-not-B error (8–12 months): Search where object was found before, not where last seen
  • Complete by 18–24 months
  • Deferred Imitation
  • Piaget: appears at 18–24 months
  • Research shows: present as early as 6 months
SLIDE 06

Challenges to Piaget's Sensorimotor Theory

  • Neo-nativism
  • Infants born with substantial innate knowledge
  • Object permanence present much earlier than Piaget proposed
  • Wynn (1992): 5-month-olds show surprise at impossible math outcomes (1+1=1)
  • Infants look longer at unexpected results, suggesting they expect 1+1 to = 2
  • Theory Theories
  • Combines neo-nativism with constructivism
  • Infants start with innate "theories" about the world
  • Test and modify theories like scientists
  • Explains both universal patterns and cultural differences
  • Research Shows
  • Deferred imitation by 6 months (not 18–24 months)
  • Object concept present very early
  • Infants are symbolic beings much earlier than Piaget thought
SLIDE 07

Preoperational Stage (2–7 Years) – Symbolic Thinking

  • Emergence of Symbolic Thought
  • Symbolic function: Using words/images to represent experiences
  • Representational insight: Knowing one thing can stand for another
  • Dual representation (DeLoache): Representing an object in two ways simultaneously
  • DeLoache's Scale Model Studies
    • 3-year-olds succeed; 2½-year-olds fail
    • Why? 2½-year-olds lack dual representation
    • Scale models are interesting objects AND symbols – harder than photos
  • Key Deficits
    • Animism: Attributing life to inanimate objects
    • Egocentrism: Difficulty seeing others' perspectives (three-mountain task)
    • Centration: Focusing on one aspect, ignoring others
    • No conservation: Don't understand properties remain despite appearance changes
    • No reversibility: Cannot mentally undo an action
SLIDE 08

Reevaluating the Preoperational Child

  • Did Piaget Underestimate Preschoolers?
  • Egocentrism: Simpler tasks show perspective-taking (Flavell's dog/cat card – 3-year-olds succeed)
  • Animism: 3-year-olds distinguish living from non-living (plants grow, tables don't heal)
  • Conservation: Identity training effective at age 4
  • False-belief tasks: 3-year-olds succeed when actively involved in deception
  • Theory of Mind (TOM)
  • Understanding that mental states guide behavior
  • Belief-desire reasoning: Behavior based on beliefs and desires
  • False-belief task: "Where will Jorge look for his chocolate?"
    • 3-year-olds: Where it really is (egocentric)
    • 4–5-year-olds: Where he thinks it is (belief-desire reasoning)
  • Social factors matter: siblings, pretend play, family conversations accelerate TOM
SLIDE 09

Concrete Operational Stage (7–11 Years)

  • New Cognitive Operations
  • Decentration: Focus on multiple aspects simultaneously
  • Reversibility: Mentally undo actions
  • Conservation: Now solve conservation problems (mass, number, liquid)
  • Mental seriation: Arrange items along dimensions (height, weight)
  • Transitivity: Understand relations in series (if A>B and B>C, then A>C)
  • Limitation
  • Thinking tied to concrete, real objects and experiences
  • Cannot reason abstractly about hypotheticals
  • Horizontal Décalage
  • Uneven cognitive performance – can solve some problems but not similar ones
  • Example: Conserve number (age 6–7) but not volume (age 9–12)
SLIDE 10

Formal Operational Stage (11+ Years)

  • Abstract and Hypothetical Reasoning
  • Hypothetico-deductive reasoning: Generate and test hypotheses
  • Inductive reasoning: Specific observations → broad generalizations
  • Can think about possibilities, not just realities
  • Three-Eye Problem
  • 9-year-olds (concrete): Place third eye on forehead; "This is stupid. Nobody has three eyes."
  • 11–12-year-olds (formal): Creative placements (palm, mouth, back of head); enjoyed the task
  • The Pendulum Problem
  • Formal operators systematically test one variable at a time
  • Discover only string length matters
  • Concrete operators test unsystematically, draw wrong conclusions
  • Individual and Cultural Variation
  • Many adults never reach formal operations
  • Expertise matters – perform better in familiar domains
  • Cultural context influences expression (Bushman hunters show formal operations on tracking tasks)
SLIDE 11

Evaluating Piaget's Theory

  • Contributions
    • Founded field of cognitive development
    • Child as active, curious explorer
    • Attempted to explain, not just describe, development
    • Broad sequences of development are accurate
    • Influenced education and other domains
  • Criticisms
  • Competence vs. Performance: Failure on tasks ≠ lack of concepts; Piaget underestimated abilities
  • Stage issue: Development not as stagelike/abrupt; inconsistency across domains
  • Vague explanations: Doesn't specify how children move between stages
  • Underestimated social/cultural influences: Child as "isolated scientist"
  • Cross-cultural evidence: Universal sequence but age variations; formal operations not universal
SLIDE 12

Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory

LevelFocus
MicrogeneticBrief periods, moment-to-moment changes
OntogeneticLifetime of individual
PhylogeneticEvolutionary time
SociohistoricalCultural history, values, technologies

  • Core Principles
    • Cognitive development occurs in sociocultural context
    • Culture shapes WHAT and HOW children think
    • Higher mental functions originate in social interaction
  • Tools of Intellectual Adaptation
    • Culturally transmitted ways of thinking
    • Example: Number-naming systems
    • Chinese: systematic base-10 (11 = "ten one")
    • English: irregular (eleven, twelve, thirteen)
    • Chinese children count to 20 earlier – not smarter, but language provides better cognitive tool
SLIDE 13

Zone of Proximal Development and Scaffolding

  • Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)
  • Range of tasks too complex to do alone but possible with guidance
  • Where new cognitive growth occurs
  • Difference between independent and assisted performance
  • Scaffolding
  • Expert tailors support to learner's current level
  • Gradually withdraws support as competence increases
  • Jointly determined by adult and child
  • Guided Participation (Rogoff)
  • Children learn through culturally relevant activities alongside skilled partners
  • Apprenticeship in thinking
  • Varies across cultures:
    • Western: Verbal instruction, praise, motivation
    • Traditional: Observation, nonverbal guidance, emulation
  • Siblings as Teachers
  • Older siblings provide detailed instruction
  • Both younger and older benefit
SLIDE 14

Language, Thought, and Educational Applications

PiagetVygotsky
Self-talkEgocentric speech reflects immaturityPrivate speech is self-guidance system
With ageDisappears with ageIncreases with task difficulty
DirectionCognitive development → languageLanguage → cognitive development
Path—Social → private → inner speech

  • Research Supports Vygotsky
    • Private speech increases on difficult tasks
    • Brighter children use more private speech
    • Improves performance after self-instruction
  • Educational Applications
    • Assess what child knows (ZPD)
    • Provide scaffolding – tailored support
    • Cooperative learning exercises
    • Teacher as facilitator, not just director
  • Freund (1990): Children who sorted furniture with mother showed dramatic improvement; alone = little improvement

  • a) Secondary circular reaction
  • b) Primary circular reaction ✓
  • c) Tertiary circular reaction
  • d) Coordinated secondary scheme

  • a) Object permanence
  • b) Representational insight
  • c) Dual representation ✓
  • d) Egocentrism

3. A child who understands that Jorge will look for his chocolate in the blue cupboard (where he left it) rather than the green cupboard (where it really is) has acquired:

  • a) Conservation
  • b) Centration
  • c) Object permanence
  • d) Belief-desire reasoning ✓

4. Vygotsky would interpret a child's self-directed muttering during a difficult task as:

  • a) Private speech serving self-guidance ✓
  • b) Egocentric speech reflecting immaturity
  • c) Evidence of cognitive disequilibrium
  • d) Failure of social perspective-taking

5. Cross-cultural research on Piaget's theory has found that:

  • a) The sequence of stages varies across cultures
  • b) Formal operations are universal by age 15
  • c) The sequence is universal but ages vary ✓
  • d) Preoperational thinking doesn't occur in some cultures
